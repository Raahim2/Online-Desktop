Paris Region - Dr. Antoine Dubois (c. 1348)

Dr. Dubois was documented visiting afflicted households in the crowded Île de la Cité and surrounding areas during the initial devastating wave of the Black Death.

Methods Employed: Focused on traditional Galenic methods like bloodletting and purging. Used aromatic herb poultices (theriac) stuffed in the beak mask, primarily believed to ward off 'miasma' or bad air.

Observations: Noted the swift progression from fever to the appearance of painful buboes in the groin and armpits, often followed by death within days.

Survival rate among his documented patients was tragically low, reflecting the era's limited understanding of contagion.

Florence Outskirts - Dr. Giovanni Rossi (c. 1349)

Records suggest Dr. Rossi primarily served communities outside Florence, possibly contracted by wealthy families who had fled the densely populated city, or tending to rural populations.

Treatment Approach: Besides standard practices, Rossi experimented with lancing buboes (a dangerous practice) and applying various plasters. Advised burning belongings of the deceased.

Key Insights: His fragmented notes mention the apparent connection between rats and the sickness, though the mechanism (fleas) was unknown. He observed higher mortality in poorer, denser settlements.

His writings, though brief, provide valuable epidemiological insights from the Italian peninsula during the Black Death's peak.

London Docklands - Dr. Thomas Ainsworth (c. 1665)

Operating during the Great Plague of London, centuries after the Black Death, Dr. Ainsworth navigated the heavily affected docklands and poorer parishes like Stepney and Whitechapel.

Iconic Attire: By this era, the plague doctor costume was more standardized. Ainsworth likely wore the full waxed canvas coat, gloves, boots, wide-brimmed hat, and the famous beak mask filled with vinegar-soaked sponges and herbs.

Public Health Role: City records mention payments to Ainsworth for his services, indicating a more formalized, municipally-funded role compared to earlier periods. He likely recorded deaths and advised on rudimentary quarantine measures ('shutting up' houses).

Despite the distinctive outfit, treatments remained largely ineffective against the bacterial infection (Yersinia pestis).

Port of Marseille - Dr. Jean Moreau (c. 1720)

Dr. Moreau served during the last major bubonic plague outbreak in Western Europe, which devastated Marseille after arriving via a merchant ship from the Levant.

Quarantine & Disinfection: Faced immense public panic and a breakdown of civic order. Moreau strongly advocated for strict maritime quarantine (lazarettos) and attempted disinfection methods using vinegar fumes, sulfur smoke, and lime washing of walls.

Challenges: Dealt with overwhelming numbers of sick and dead, often supervising mass burials. His detailed clinical accounts emphasized the importance of isolating the sick, though enforcement proved extremely difficult amidst the chaos.

Moreau's work highlighted the growing understanding of contagion and the critical role of public health measures, even if the specific vector was still debated.

Central Europe - Generic Route (Various Periods)

Plague doctors, often second-rate physicians or young doctors seeking experience, traveled through various towns and cities in Central Europe (Holy Roman Empire, Poland, etc.) during recurrent outbreaks from the 14th to 18th centuries.

Contracts & Compensation: They often negotiated contracts with town councils, receiving stipends, housing, and sometimes special privileges. Their duties included treating the afflicted (rich and poor), tallying deaths, and occasionally performing autopsies (often against church doctrine).

Varied Practices: Methods varied widely, influenced by local medical traditions and the specific doctor's training. Superstition and religious beliefs often intertwined with medical advice.

These itinerant doctors were often regarded with a mixture of fear and hope by the communities they served.
